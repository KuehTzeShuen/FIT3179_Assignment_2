ALCOHOL CONSUMPTION STATISTICS

Alcohol Consumption per Capita

The map shows the average alcohol consumption in liters of pure alcohol among people aged 15 and older for the year 2018. Higher alcohol consumption is observed across Europe, Russia, Australia, Canada, and parts of South America, while lower levels appear in Africa, the Middle East, and parts of Asia. This map shows a significant variation in drinking habits across different regions of the world. Even thoough the map shows a global perspective per capita, it is clear that economic status is not the only factor that affects drinking patterns. Local cultural, social, and demographic factors influence alcohol consumption patterns as well.

Australia's alcohol consumption per capita is around 10.51 liters , placing it above the OECD average of 8.6 liters. This makes Australia one of the highest consumers of alcohol globally, marking it as a significant part of the country's social and cultural practices.

Alcohol Consumption in Australia

The multi-line chart narrows our scope from a global scale to show trends in the average liters of alcohol consumed per person in Australia specifically, broken down by beverage type (Beer, Wine, Spirits, and Cider), along with total alcohol consumption. From 1980 to 2018, total alcohol consumption per capita in Australia shows a general decline, falling from over 12.72 liters in the early 1980 to 9.51 liters by 2021. Beer consumption, which dominated in the 1980s at around 8 liters per capita, decreased steadily over time to around 4 liters in present times. Wine consumption, on the other hand, gradually increased, especially from the 1980s onward, and is set to overtake beer consumption at the current rates. Spirits consumption rose slightly but remained moderate throughout the period at around 2 liters, while cider consumption only began in Australia in 2003 according to this dataset.

Overall, the data suggests a long-term shift in Australians drinking habits from beer toward wine and other beverages, and a gradual reduction in total alcohol intake over the decades. This marks a cultural shift towards prioritising health and wellness, which has become the topic of many public health campaigns in recent years.

Proportion of adults in Australia who exceeded guideline by age, 2022

Despite the decline in overall alcohol consumption, alcoholism (or binge drinking) still remains a big issue in Australia. The chart presents the percentage of Australian adults who consumed alcohol at levels exceeding the national health guidelines, grouped by age. The data reveals a strong age-related pattern. The highest proportion of adults exceeding the guideline occurs among those aged 18-24, with roughly one-third of individuals in this group drinking above the recommended levels. The proportions then generally decline with age. Adults aged 25-34 and 35-44 also show notable rates of excess consumption, while older age groups, particularly those aged 65 years and over, are less likely to exceed the guideline. This pattern suggests that younger adults are more likely to engage in risky drinking behaviors compared with older populations, highlighting the importance of targeted public health interventions for early adult age groups.

Alcohol Consumption Guideline Exceedance by Sex in Australia

Breaking it down even further, the diverging bar chart shows the proportion of Australian adults exceeding alcohol consumption guidelines in 2022 by sex. Across nearly all age groups, males consistently outnumber females in exceeding the guideline, indicating higher rates of risky drinking among men. The largest numbers of exceedances occur in the 25-34, 35-44 and over 65 age groups for males, and 25-34 and 45-54 in females, reflecting peak drinking years. In contrast, the 15-17 and 18-24 age groups show a lot lower levels of exceedance. Overall, the chart highlights a clear gender disparity in alcohol consumption patterns, with men more likely to drink beyond recommended limits. All in all, these insights underscore the need for more public awareness on responsible drinking habits, especially among younger adults to maintain the current trends, as even though overall alcohol consumption is declining, risky drinking behaviors are still a persistent occurance in Australia.
